The World’s Highest Environment

Mount Everest is one of the most extreme natural environments on Earth. Located in the Himalayas between Nepal and Tibet, it reaches an altitude of 8,848.86 meters above sea level, where atmospheric pressure and oxygen levels drop to dangerously low levels.

This environment is so harsh that the human body begins to lose performance rapidly, making survival extremely difficult without specialized equipment.

Extreme Environmental Conditions

Temperatures on Everest can fall below -60°C during winter storms, while wind speeds can exceed hurricane levels. These conditions make exposed skin vulnerable within minutes.

At higher altitudes, oxygen levels are only about one-third of those at sea level. This condition forces climbers to rely on supplemental oxygen systems to survive prolonged exposure.

Geological Formation

Mount Everest is part of the Himalayan mountain range, formed by the ongoing collision between the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates. This geological process continues today, causing the mountain to rise slowly each year.

Scientists estimate Everest to be between 50 and 60 million years old, making it relatively young in geological terms compared to other mountain systems around the world.

Wildlife and Ecosystem

The lower regions of Everest support a fragile ecosystem that includes species such as snow leopards, Himalayan tahr, red pandas, and yaks. These animals are specially adapted to survive in cold and oxygen-limited environments.

However, above 6,000 meters, life becomes nearly impossible due to extreme cold, lack of oxygen, and constant exposure to harsh weather conditions. This region is considered biologically uninhabitable.
