Extreme Conditions

Mount Everest is one of the most extreme environments on Earth. Temperatures can drop below -60°C, and wind speeds can become dangerously high, creating conditions that are extremely difficult for human survival.

At high altitude, oxygen levels are extremely low—only about one-third of what is available at sea level. Because of this, climbers must use supplemental oxygen to survive in the upper regions of the mountain.

Geological Facts

Mount Everest continues to grow slowly every year due to tectonic activity caused by the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates. This movement forces the Himalayas upward over time.

Scientifically, Everest is estimated to be around 50 to 60 million years old, making it relatively young in geological terms compared to many other mountain formations on Earth.

Wildlife and Environment

Lower regions of Everest are home to unique wildlife such as snow leopards, red pandas, and Himalayan yaks. These animals are adapted to cold environments and high altitudes.

However, above 6,000 meters, almost no life can survive due to extreme cold, lack of oxygen, and harsh weather conditions. This area is considered biologically uninhabitable.
