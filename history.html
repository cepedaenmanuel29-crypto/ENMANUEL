The Discovery and Early Exploration

Mount Everest was first identified as the highest mountain in the world in the 19th century during the Great Trigonometrical Survey of India. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} At that time, it was called "Peak XV" before being renamed after Sir George Everest.

Early explorers faced extreme difficulties, including lack of proper equipment, unknown terrain, and harsh weather conditions. Many early expeditions failed, and some climbers disappeared while attempting to reach the summit.

The First Successful Ascent

On May 29, 1953, Sir Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay became the first climbers to successfully reach the summit of Mount Everest. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4} Their achievement marked one of the greatest milestones in human history.

They used oxygen tanks, ropes, and teamwork to overcome extreme conditions. Their success opened the door for future climbers and transformed Everest into a global destination.

Modern Climbing Era

Since the 1990s, Everest has become more accessible due to commercial expeditions. Thousands of climbers have attempted the summit, and over 13,000 successful climbs have been recorded. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

However, this popularity has also led to overcrowding, environmental damage, and increased risks on the mountain.
