The Discovery of Everest

Mount Everest was identified as the highest mountain in the world during the Great Trigonometrical Survey of India in the 19th century. At the time, it was known as “Peak XV” before being renamed in honor of Sir George Everest, a British surveyor who contributed to mapping the region.

Much of the Himalayas remained unexplored during this period. Maps were incomplete, and the region was considered one of the most remote and difficult places on Earth to access.

Early Exploration Attempts

The first expeditions to Everest faced extreme conditions without modern equipment or proper knowledge of high-altitude survival. Climbers relied on basic gear, physical endurance, and local Sherpa guides.

Many early attempts failed due to avalanches, extreme cold, and altitude sickness. Some expeditions ended in tragedy, highlighting the unpredictable power of the mountain.

Despite this, interest in Everest continued to grow, driven by human curiosity and the desire to reach the highest point on Earth.

The First Successful Ascent (1953)

On May 29, 1953, Sir Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay became the first climbers to reach the summit of Mount Everest. This historic achievement marked a turning point in human exploration.

The expedition required careful planning, teamwork, oxygen systems, and the support of many Sherpas who played a crucial role in the climb.

This moment transformed Everest into a global symbol of human determination and exploration.

The Modern Climbing Era

In recent decades, Mount Everest has become more accessible due to technological advances and commercial expedition services. Thousands of climbers now attempt the summit every year.

However, this popularity has created new challenges, including overcrowding, environmental damage, and increased safety risks.

Despite these issues, Everest remains one of the most respected and dangerous mountains in the world.

Cultural and Environmental Significance

Mount Everest holds deep cultural and spiritual meaning for the people of Nepal and Tibet. It is known locally as Sagarmatha and Chomolungma and is considered sacred.

Today, conservation efforts aim to reduce waste and protect the fragile Himalayan ecosystem from the impact of tourism and climbing activity.
