The Discovery and Early Exploration

Mount Everest was first identified as the highest mountain on Earth during the Great Trigonometrical Survey of India in the 19th century. At that time, it was known as “Peak XV” before being officially renamed in honor of Sir George Everest, a British surveyor who contributed to mapping the region.

During the early stages of exploration, very little was known about the Himalayas. Maps were incomplete, weather conditions were unpredictable, and equipment was extremely limited compared to modern standards. Early explorers relied on basic tools, physical endurance, and local guides to navigate the unknown terrain.

Many of these early expeditions failed due to harsh conditions, including extreme cold, avalanches, and lack of oxygen at high altitudes. Some climbers were never seen again, highlighting the danger and mystery surrounding the mountain during that period.

The First Successful Ascent

On May 29, 1953, history was made when Sir Edmund Hillary from New Zealand and Tenzing Norgay, a Sherpa from Nepal, became the first climbers to successfully reach the summit of Mount Everest. This achievement marked a turning point in human exploration.

Their expedition was carefully planned and supported by a large team of climbers and Sherpas. They used oxygen equipment, ropes, and specialized gear to survive the extreme conditions of the mountain.

Reaching the summit was not only a physical achievement but also a symbol of human determination, teamwork, and the ability to overcome natural limits. Their success opened the door for future mountaineering expeditions around the world.

The Rise of Modern Climbing

Since the late 20th century, Mount Everest has become increasingly popular among climbers. Advances in technology, improved equipment, and better weather forecasting have made expeditions more accessible than in the past.

However, this increase in popularity has also created new challenges. Overcrowding on climbing routes, environmental pollution, and safety risks have become serious concerns for authorities managing the mountain.

Despite these challenges, Everest continues to attract thousands of climbers each year, each hoping to experience one of the greatest adventures on Earth.

Impact on Culture and Environment

Mount Everest is not only a natural wonder but also an important cultural symbol for the people of Nepal and Tibet. Locally known as Sagarmatha and Chomolungma, the mountain holds spiritual significance and is respected as a sacred place.

At the same time, increased tourism and climbing activity have had environmental impacts, including waste accumulation and pressure on natural resources. Conservation efforts are now in place to protect the mountain for future generations.
